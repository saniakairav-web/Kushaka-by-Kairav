The Complete Lore

The Fall of Kushaka

"Seven kings. One curse. One hundred years. One night that ended everything."

I
Chapter One
The Curse Before the City

Before Kushaka had a name — before its first stone was laid or its first torch was lit — something ancient stirred in the heart of the sun. Not a god. Not a demon. Something older than both. A being that had spent millennia consuming civilizations the way flame consumes paper: completely, silently, and without leaving anything behind. Those who survived long enough to whisper of it called it only The Harbinger — the Symbol of Fear given form, or rather, given shadow.

Centuries before Kushaka was imagined, the Harbinger sensed a pulse from deep within the Earth. Raw. Ancient. Buried. A power so extraordinary that it stopped the entity cold — something it had never felt across its entire existence. A Cosmic Relic, older than any civilization, sleeping beneath layers of rock and forgotten time. The Harbinger craved it immediately. But it was dying — weakened from its last great consumption — and could not cross the void to Earth. Not yet.

So instead of attacking, it did something that no enemy of the Harbinger had ever survived: it was patient. It entered hibernation inside the sun's molten core, sealed itself in heat and silence for a hundred years — and before it closed its eyes, it reached across space with the last of its waning power and planted a curse. Not on a place. Not on a person. On greed itself. On the specific, uniquely human hunger that never fills. Then it slept, trusting that one century would do what it could not.

II
Chapter Two
The Seven Kings of Kushaka

The curse landed like a seed in dark soil and over generations found exactly what it needed: seven men. Seven hollow, frightened men who wore gold crowns because crowns made them feel less afraid. The Seven Kings of Kushaka had inherited both their thrones and their curse from ancestors who had stumbled upon a ritual centuries before the city existed — a blood moon ceremony. Gold cast into black flame. Words spoken in a dead tongue. In return: health, wealth, dominion. A kingdom that never crumbled.

Their ancestors celebrated it as divine favour. Their fathers performed it without question. And the seven kings continued it, every blood moon, every year — because the alternative was unthinkable. They had each felt the ritual's pull: a pressure behind the eyes on blood moon nights, a voice inside their skulls that was not theirs. They were afraid of it. But the gold kept flowing, the city kept thriving, and frightened men who hold power will sacrifice anything except the power itself.

What no one understood — not the kings, not their scholars, not their priests — was that every ceremony was not a prayer. It was a heartbeat. Each blood moon fed the Harbinger's hibernation, kept the curse deepening into the city's soil, and silently counted down the years until the entity would wake and collect what it was owed. The kings were not rulers. They were instruments. And they had been playing the Harbinger's song for generations without once knowing the words.

Meanwhile, Kushaka itself had become extraordinary. A city of hundreds of thousands. Luminous towers and humming markets. Scholars mapping stars that hadn't been named yet. And at its heart — the Aegisnights, the last of an ancient warrior bloodline. Incorruptible. Fearless. Noble to the bone. They served the city with their lives, completely unaware of what rotted on the throne above them.

III
Chapter Three
The Relic Found

Deep beneath Kushaka, miners were doing what miners do — digging for gold to feed the city's ever-hungry economy. Three hundred feet underground, on one particular dig, the earth cracked differently. The air turned cold. And a pickaxe struck something that rang not like mineral, but like a single note of music played at the very beginning of the universe.

They pulled it free: a crystal, deep violet at its core, warm to the touch despite the frozen dark around it. When it reached the surface and caught daylight, it didn't merely reflect the light — it remembered it, as if the sun recognized an old friend and leaned down. The miners, understanding only that this was beyond them, passed it upward through the chain of command until it reached Commander Seraphine Drael — the last surviving commander of the Aegisnights, a woman forged from discipline and iron resolve, bearing the scars of every battle she had fought for Kushaka on her armour and her face.

She held the crystal and felt something ancient turn toward her. She didn't understand it. But she felt — with a soldier's absolute certainty — that it belonged to the city. With a general's instinct and a protector's heart, she placed the Cosmic Relic at the Heart Spire, the great tower at Kushaka's centre. She believed she was doing something good. She was right, and catastrophically wrong, at the same time.

The transformation was immediate. Crops doubled. Disease vanished. The nights grew luminous. Children were born stronger. The elderly felt years lift. The whole city became more itself than it had ever been — radiant, alive, electric with an energy that had no earthly source. The people celebrated for weeks. The kings accepted the praise. And no one thought to ask what the crystal truly was. Happiness rarely invites investigation.

IV
Chapter Four
The Harbinger Wakes

In the sun's core, the hibernation ended. The Cosmic Relic — no longer buried, no longer sleeping, blazing from the centre of a city of three hundred thousand — was a beacon that reached across the void between worlds. And the Harbinger opened its eyes for the first time in a century.

That same night, the seven kings woke screaming from identical nightmares. The same image in every skull: a figure made of compressed darkness, wearing no face, standing at the foot of their beds. And a voice that came from inside their own minds: "The contract is due. Bring me the relic before the third blood moon. Fail — and I will not take your lives. I will take your selves."

They understood with cold, gut-twisting clarity what the ritual had always been. Not a blessing. A leash. A hundred years of blood moons had built a debt, and the payment wasn't death — death would have been mercy. It was erasure. Their consciousness consumed. Their identities dissolved. Left behind: hollow vessels wearing their faces, walking in their shadows, serving the god they had unknowingly spent generations waking up. The Harbinger did not want to kill the kings. It wanted to wear them.

V
Chapter Five
The Betrayal

Cornered, terrified, stripped of everything except desperation, the seven kings appeared before their people — and made their demand publicly. Give us the crystal. Immediately. No explanation. No negotiation. The crowd went silent the way crowds do before they become something dangerous.

Then Commander Seraphine Drael stepped forward from the Aegisnights' ranks and looked at the kings with an expression colder than rage. She had suspected something was rotten in the throne room for years. She had never had proof. Now the kings were providing it themselves, in public, in front of the entire city. She refused. One word. Quiet. Absolute. And in that single refusal — spoken with the devastating calm of a warrior who knows exactly what she is choosing and chooses it anyway — the people of Kushaka found their voice. They refused too.

The kings, faced with the one thing they feared more than the Harbinger — losing control — commanded the execution of the Aegisnights. And in doing so, committed the final, unforgivable act: they turned Kushaka against itself. Steel against steel. Crown against city. For one bloody night, the civilization that had been built in love tore itself apart in betrayal. But none of it had ever mattered. Because while Seraphine fought on the steps of the Heart Spire and the kings hid behind their guards, the shadows simply began to move wrong.

VI
Chapter Six
The Harbinger Arrives

There was no grand entrance. No announcement. No moment to prepare. The darkness pooled where geometry said it couldn't. Torches snuffed without wind. And then, between one heartbeat and the next, the Harbinger was simply there — a figure-shaped compression of reality, bending the air around it the way heat bends light, wearing darkness the way a wound wears dried blood.

The kings saw it and ran. Not tactically. Not strategically. They ran — stumbling, falling over each other, seven golden crowns hitting bloodstained stone — and every pretence of power dissolved in an instant. The Harbinger moved through the chaos like smoke through a keyhole and ascended the Heart Spire. The Cosmic Relic reached toward it. The connection between them was so ancient and absolute that the ground shook with the recognition of it. The sky above Kushaka cracked open — not sunset red, not fire red, but the specific red of something that has been waiting a very long time and has finally arrived.

The Harbinger looked out over the city — all its beauty, all its chaos, all its hundreds of thousands of warm and terrified lives — and made a decision rooted in something colder than hatred. Utility. Kushaka was not worth destroying. It was worth using. The people had served one purpose in growing over the Relic. Now they would serve another.

VII
Chapter Seven
The Bleed

It began as a sound. A frequency no instrument could measure — but every body felt it in the marrow, behind the eyes, in the chest cavity like a second heartbeat that belonged to something else entirely. Then the shadows detached.

The Bleed was not a disease. It was the Cosmic Relic's power weaponized — a corruption that dissolved the boundary between a living person and the darkness inside them. Those infected did not die. They transformed. Their shadows peeled away and grew, consuming them from the feet upward, replacing flesh with dark matter and memory with hunger. What remained were the Shadow-Broken — creatures wearing the faces of the people they used to be, hunting by the sound of heartbeats, driven by an instinct that was neither human nor animal but something entirely new and terrible.

Within one night, half of Kushaka was gone. The remaining Aegisnights fought with everything they had. But you cannot cut a shadow with iron. By dawn, the city that had stood for a century lay silent — not in ruins, but occupied. Transformed. The greatest civilization of its age, swallowed by the dark it had unknowingly been growing inside itself all along.

VIII
Chapter Eight — You Begin Here
Astra

You were thirteen years old when the Bleed came. You remember your mother's voice the last time she said your name — Astra — in that particular tone she used when she was proud and afraid at the same time. You remember your father standing between you and the spreading dark with nothing but his body and everything he had. You remember the silence after. You don't remember how you survived the night. You shouldn't have. No one should have.

But you did. And when you came back to consciousness in the ruins of Kushaka — red sky cracking overhead, Shadow-Broken moving through streets you used to run as a child — something was different. The Bleed had touched you and stopped. As if something in your blood had refused it. As if you were meant for a purpose the Harbinger had not accounted for.

You have no army. No throne. No ancient bloodline. You are one person in a broken city, carrying nothing except the memory of two faces and the weight of love that has been turned into grief. But grief — real grief, the kind that doesn't perform itself — is not weakness. It is fuel.

The Harbinger made one mistake.
It left you breathing.

Characters of Kushaka

Protagonist · Player Character
Astra Vael
"The boy who should be dead — and isn't."
Thirteen years old. No title, no bloodline, no army. The Bleed touched him and stopped — something in his blood resists the corruption that consumed everyone he loved. He carries two memories and one purpose: make the Harbinger answer for what it took.
Commander · Last Aegisknight
Seraphine Drael
"She saw the kings for what they were before anyone else dared to look."
The last commander of the Aegisnights. Iron-willed, battle-scarred, unbreakable. She placed the Relic in the Heart Spire believing it was a gift to her city. That decision haunts her more than any wound. Now half-consumed by the Bleed, she fights not to win — but to make sure Astra can.
The Entity · Final Antagonist
The Harbinger
"It does not hate you. It simply doesn't see you as significant."
Ancient beyond reckoning. It has consumed dozens of civilizations and remembers none of their names. Kushaka was a resource. The Bleed is an experiment. Astra is an anomaly it intends to correct. In a hundred centuries, it has never once been wrong about the outcome.
Antagonists · The Fallen
The Seven Kings
"Cowards in golden armour who mistook inheritance for choice."
They did not choose the curse — they inherited it. But they chose silence over confession, greed over warning, and preservation of their crowns over the lives of three hundred thousand people. Now scattered in the ruins — some Shadow-Broken, some merely broken. Not worthy of hatred. Barely worthy of pity.
Enemies · The Transformed
The Shadow-Broken
"They wear the faces of the people Astra used to know."
Not monsters born from darkness — people transformed by the Bleed. Miners. Scholars. Soldiers. Neighbours. They remember nothing of who they were. But sometimes, in rare flickering moments, something almost like recognition crosses a Shadow-Broken face. Those are the worst moments in the game.

Protagonist · Player Character · Survivor
Astra Vael
"I don't need to be the strongest person in Kushaka. I just need to be the last one standing when this ends."
Age

Thirteen years old at the time of the Bleed. Age unknown after.

Status

Survivor. The only person the Bleed touched and released.

Bloodline

Unknown. No royal lineage. No warrior ancestry on record. The anomaly in his blood has no identified origin.

Motivation

Vengeance. Memory. The refusal to let his parents' deaths mean nothing.

His Story

Astra Vael was an ordinary boy in an extraordinary city. The son of craftspeople, raised in the lower district of Kushaka where the lanterns stayed on all night and the markets never fully slept. He grew up knowing the Aegisnights by sight — recognizing Seraphine Drael's silhouette in the early morning patrols, feeling safe in a city that seemed like it would last forever.

When the Bleed came, Astra lost everything in the span of a single night. His mother. His father. His street. His city. The darkness moved through his home like water through paper, and something in him — some unidentifiable, nameless quality in his blood — resisted it. The Bleed tried to take him and stopped. Left him breathing. Left him alone in a city full of shadows wearing familiar faces.

He woke with a choice that wasn't really a choice at all: survive and do nothing, or survive and matter. Astra chose the latter not out of heroism — he would be the first to tell you that — but out of the simple, devastating logic of someone who has lost everything and realised that grief without direction is just slow death.

His immunity to the Bleed makes him uniquely able to move through Kushaka's infected streets. His youth makes him underestimated. His grief makes him dangerous. The Harbinger catalogued him as an anomaly and filed him under problems to be corrected. That was its first mistake. Underestimating what a thirteen-year-old with nothing left to lose is capable of — that was its last.

Commander · Last of the Aegisnights · Mentor
Seraphine Drael
"I placed the Relic in the Spire. I thought I was giving the city a gift. I think about that every single second."
Title

Commander of the Aegisnights. Last of the Drael bloodline.

Status

Wounded. Partially Bleed-touched. Fighting with borrowed time and refusing to waste a second of it.

Defining Trait

She refused the kings' order in front of the entire city. That single word — "No" — cost her everything she had built and she would say it again without hesitation.

Role in the Game

Mentor. Tactician. Protector. The person who makes sure Astra survives long enough to become who he needs to be.

Her Story

Seraphine Drael spent twenty years earning the title of Commander. She fought in border wars that historians would spend decades debating. She outlived three predecessors and a dozen enemies who had been certain, at one point or another, that they had her. She was not invincible — her armour is a record of every time she almost wasn't — but she was consistent in a way that mattered more: she always got back up.

When the miners brought the crystal up from underground, it was Seraphine who made the call to place it in the Heart Spire. In retrospect, that decision haunts her more than any battle wound. She had felt something when she held the Relic — a recognition she couldn't name — and she had interpreted it as belonging. As rightness. She was not wrong about the feeling. She was catastrophically wrong about what it meant.

When the kings made their demand at the spire, Seraphine had already been watching them for years — cataloguing small wrongnesses that never quite assembled into proof. In that moment, they assembled all at once. She refused. And in refusing, she gave the people of Kushaka a blueprint for what courage looked like: quiet, certain, and entirely without self-preservation.

The Bleed reached Seraphine during the battle but couldn't fully take her — the Cosmic Relic's energy, which she had held and placed, left something in her blood that resists the corruption even as it encroaches. She survives the fall of Kushaka in fragments, holding herself together through discipline and the specific, unflinching stubbornness of someone who refuses to die before they have fixed what they helped break.

She finds Astra in the ruins. She recognises, immediately, what his immunity means. She stops fighting to survive. She starts fighting to give him time.

The Entity · The Symbol of Fear · Final Antagonist
The Harbinger
"It has no interest in your suffering. It has no interest in you at all. That is what makes it monstrous."
True Name

Veyrath. A name so old it predates the language it belongs to. The Harbinger does not use it. It has no need of names.

Age

Unknown. It was ancient when the oldest human civilizations were new.

Nature

It does not think of itself as evil. It thinks of civilizations as resources. It thinks of Astra as a variable. It has not yet thought of him as a threat.

Weakness

Unknown — to everyone, including itself. Whatever is in Astra's blood that resists the Bleed is something the Harbinger has never encountered. It does not know what it doesn't know.

What It Is

The Harbinger is not a conqueror. Conquerors want to rule. The Harbinger wants to consume — to take the energy of civilizations and use it to sustain itself, endlessly, across centuries. Kushaka was not its first. It will not be its last, if Astra fails.

It does not hate humanity. Hatred requires consideration, and the Harbinger does not consider humans significant enough to feel strongly about. They are ecosystems. Resources. The Bleed is not an act of cruelty — it is an act of harvesting. The Shadow-Broken are not monsters created out of malice. They are byproducts. Waste material repurposed into tools.

The curse it planted on greed was not personal. It was simply the most efficient mechanism available. Greed is reliable. Greed is patient. Greed will always find a ritual, always perform it, always repeat it, always feed whatever needs feeding. The Harbinger understood this because it spent enough time observing humanity to learn its most consistent truth: people will do almost anything if you make the doing feel like prosperity.

What it does not understand — what it has never had to understand in centuries of identical conquests — is what happens when a person has nothing left to lose. It catalogued Astra as an anomaly. It filed him away as a variable to be corrected. It turned its attention to the larger project of processing Kushaka and never looked back. That is its flaw. That is the only flaw it has ever had. It has never once, across a hundred civilizations, left a witness.

Antagonists · The Cursed Rulers · The Fallen
The Seven Kings
"They were not born evil. They were born afraid — and given just enough power to make it everyone else's problem."
Collective Title

The Seven Kings of Kushaka. Rulers of the most powerful city of their era.

The Eldest King

Mordechai — the most terrified of the seven and therefore the most dangerous. Every atrocity the kings committed was his idea first.

Their Crime

Not the curse — they inherited that. Their crime was silence. A hundred years of choosing comfort over confession.

Their Fate

Scattered. Some consumed by the Bleed. Some wandering the ruins as shadows of men. None of them ruling anything. Not one of them has removed their crown.

Who They Were

The easiest thing in the world would be to call the Seven Kings simply evil and move on. But the most honest and most disturbing truth about them is this: they were ordinary men who inherited an extraordinary problem and chose, at every available fork in the road, the path that preserved their comfort. That choice, repeated enough times across enough years, became evil. Not in a dramatic moment. In the accumulation of silences.

They knew — or suspected — that the blood moon ritual was something darker than tradition. The voice behind the eyes grew louder every year. The ritual's demands occasionally shifted in ways that required them to do things they preferred not to remember. They had scholars who asked uncomfortable questions, and those scholars had a habit of disappearing. They had advisors who suggested transparency, and those advisors had a habit of being reassigned to distant posts.

They told themselves they were protecting the city. That the people were better off not knowing. That ignorance was a form of kindness. This is what cowardice always tells itself to sleep at night.

When the Harbinger gave them their ultimatum, they revealed themselves completely — not as villains who had always been plotting, but as men who had always been scared and had simply run out of room to hide it. They commanded Seraphine's execution not out of cruelty but out of panic. They ran when the Harbinger arrived not out of strategy but out of the same reflex that had driven every decision they'd ever made. In the end, all seven of them chose themselves. They always had.

Enemies · The Transformed · The Lost
Shadow-Broken
"They are not your enemies. They were your neighbours. That is the whole point."
Origin

Every Shadow-Broken was a Kushakan citizen. Miners. Merchants. Children. Soldiers. The Bleed took them all without distinction.

Nature

Dark matter and fractured memory. They hunt by heartbeat. They do not feel pain. They do not feel anything — except, occasionally, something almost like recognition.

Notable Variant

Nyxara — a Shadow-Broken who retained partial consciousness. Neither fully human nor fully consumed. Potentially the most dangerous entity in Kushaka.

Weakness

The same quality in Astra's blood that resists the Bleed can, under the right conditions, reach the last remnant of a Shadow-Broken's original self. Whether that is mercy or weapon has yet to be determined.

What They Are

The Harbinger designed the Bleed to be efficient. It needed Kushaka's population converted into mobile, self-sustaining instruments for processing the Cosmic Relic's energy. The Shadow-Broken are the result: people repurposed. The body, transformed. The memory, consumed. What remains is capable of movement, of hunting, of receiving and carrying out the Harbinger's ambient will without requiring instruction.

What the Harbinger did not anticipate — because it had never needed to before — was that some people are difficult to fully erase. Their identity is too dense, too defined, too deeply themselves. In most cases the Bleed simply overpowers this. But in rare instances — in people of extraordinary will, or in those who had contact with the Cosmic Relic's energy — the transformation is incomplete.

Nyxara was a senior Aegisknight who fell in the battle at the Heart Spire. The Bleed reached her during a moment when she was physically touching the Relic's plinth, and the collision of energies created something that has no precedent: a Shadow-Broken who remembers her name. Who knows what she was. Who understands, in her fractured way, what she has become — and feels something about it that she cannot yet name but that looks, from certain angles, like rage.

She is not an ally. She is not quite an enemy. She is a question the game will spend significant time asking: if the transformation can be incomplete, can it be reversed? And if it can — should it be, when what you would bring back into the world is a warrior who has been through what Nyxara has been through?

Core Themes
The ideas that make Kushaka more than a story about a monster
Inherited Evil

The kings didn't choose the curse. They inherited it — and chose silence. The game asks: at what point does complicity become evil? How many blood moons does it take for an inherited wrong to become a chosen one?

Grief as Fuel

Astra's strength comes not from training or destiny but from love that was taken and the refusal to let that mean nothing. Grief, properly channelled, is the most sustainable source of power in the game's world.

Indifferent Evil

The Harbinger is not evil the way storybook villains are evil. It simply does not see humanity as significant. That indifference — the knowledge that you are not even worth hating — is far more terrifying than malice.

Corruption & Identity

Shadow-Broken wear the faces of people Astra knew. Every enemy encounter carries emotional weight. Every battle in Kushaka is also, in some sense, a funeral — and Astra is always the only mourner.

Good Intentions

Seraphine placing the Relic in the Spire was an act of pure good faith. The kings performing the ritual were, at first, simply continuing tradition. The road to Kushaka's fall is paved entirely with comprehensible decisions.

The City as Character

Kushaka's beauty — its towers, its markets, its light — is not backdrop. It is a character that the audience comes to love before they have to watch it fall. The loss must be felt. The loss must matter.

One hundred years of blood moons. One night of consequences. One boy standing in the wreckage with nothing to lose and everything to prove.
— The World of Kushaka
